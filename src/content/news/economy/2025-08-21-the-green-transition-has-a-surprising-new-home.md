## The Green Transition Has a Surprising New Home

As the global community grapples with the pressing challenges of climate change and environmental degradation, the green transition—characterized by a shift towards sustainable practices and renewable energy—has emerged as a focal point for many nations. While northern European countries have often been lauded for their progressive environmental policies, recent developments indicate that other regions are stepping into the spotlight as leaders in the green transition.

### A Shift in Perception

Historically, countries like Denmark, Sweden, and the Netherlands have been recognized for their commitment to sustainability, boasting coalition governments that prioritize environmental issues and a culture that embraces cycling and public transportation. However, the narrative is evolving, and a new wave of countries is making significant strides in the green transition.

### Emerging Leaders

Recent reports highlight that nations in the Global South, particularly in Africa and Latin America, are increasingly adopting innovative approaches to sustainability. Countries such as Kenya and Costa Rica have implemented ambitious renewable energy initiatives and conservation strategies that not only address local environmental challenges but also contribute to global sustainability goals.

For instance, Kenya has made remarkable progress in harnessing geothermal energy, positioning itself as a leader in renewable energy in Africa. The country now derives a substantial portion of its electricity from geothermal sources, reducing reliance on fossil fuels and lowering carbon emissions. Similarly, Costa Rica has garnered international acclaim for its commitment to reforestation and biodiversity conservation, achieving a remarkable feat of running on 99% renewable energy for several consecutive years.

### The Role of Technology

Technological advancements are playing a pivotal role in facilitating the green transition in these emerging nations. Innovations in solar energy, waste management, and sustainable agriculture are being adopted at an unprecedented rate. For example, mobile technology is enabling farmers in rural areas to access information about sustainable practices, enhancing productivity while minimizing environmental impact.

Moreover, international partnerships and investments are bolstering these efforts. Organizations and governments from developed nations are increasingly collaborating with their counterparts in the Global South, providing funding and expertise to support green initiatives. This collaborative approach not only accelerates the transition but also fosters economic growth and job creation in these regions.

### The Importance of Inclusivity

As the green transition unfolds, it is crucial to ensure that it is inclusive and equitable. The benefits of sustainable practices should extend to all communities, particularly those that have historically been marginalized. Engaging local populations in decision-making processes and providing access to green technologies can empower communities and enhance resilience against climate change.

### Conclusion

The green transition is no longer confined to the northern European model that has long dominated discussions on sustainability. As countries in the Global South rise to prominence in this arena, the global narrative is shifting. The experiences and innovations emerging from these regions offer valuable insights into how diverse approaches can contribute to a more sustainable future. As the world continues to confront the realities of climate change, it is imperative that all nations, regardless of their geographical or economic status, work collaboratively towards a greener, more sustainable planet.
