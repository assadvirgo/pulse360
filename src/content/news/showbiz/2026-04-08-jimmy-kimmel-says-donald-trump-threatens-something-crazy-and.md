## Jimmy Kimmel Comments on Trump's Negotiating Tactics Regarding Iran

In a recent episode of "Jimmy Kimmel Live!", host Jimmy Kimmel offered his insights on the ongoing ceasefire negotiations between the United States and Iran, highlighting a notable pattern in President Donald Trump's approach to diplomacy. Kimmel's remarks came during his Tuesday night monologue, where he humorously critiqued the President's decision-making process.

### Ceasefire Negotiations with Iran

Kimmel began by addressing the current situation concerning the ceasefire between the United States and Iran. He noted that President Trump has opted to delay any military action against Iran for at least two weeks. This decision, according to Kimmel, aligns with a broader trend in Trump's negotiating style, which often involves a combination of threats and subsequent delays.

### A Pattern of Threats and Delays

During the monologue, Kimmel remarked, “The President decided not to drop it for at least another two weeks,” suggesting that Trump’s approach can be likened to that of a “conscientious employee.” This characterization implies that while Trump may make bold threats, he often follows them with a period of reflection or postponement, allowing for more time to assess the situation. Kimmel's comments reflect a common perception among political commentators that Trump's tactics can be unpredictable yet methodical.

### Public Reaction and Implications

Kimmel's humorous take on the situation resonated with his audience, who are often engaged in the broader political discourse surrounding Trump's presidency. By framing the President's actions in a comedic light, Kimmel invites viewers to consider the implications of such negotiating tactics on international relations. The humor serves to underscore the seriousness of the situation while also making it accessible to a wider audience.

### Conclusion

As the United States navigates its complex relationship with Iran, Kimmel's observations highlight the intricate dynamics of presidential decision-making in matters of foreign policy.
