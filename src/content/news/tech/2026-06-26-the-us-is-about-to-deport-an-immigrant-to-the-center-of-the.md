In a troubling development, the U.S. government is preparing to deport Edward Nalwamba, a 78-year-old retired pastor, to a region experiencing a resurgence of the Ebola virus. This decision has raised significant concerns among his advocates and health experts, particularly given Nalwamba's deteriorating health while in detention.

### Background on Edward Nalwamba

Edward Nalwamba, originally from the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), has been residing in the United States for several years. His attorney has reported that Nalwamba's health has rapidly declined during his time in detention, prompting fears about his ability to cope with the challenges he may face upon his return to a country grappling with an ongoing Ebola crisis.

### Health Concerns and Legal Challenges

Nalwamba's attorney has voiced serious concerns regarding his client's health, arguing that deportation could be life-threatening. The attorney has highlighted that Nalwamba suffers from various health issues that have worsened during his detention, including potential complications related to his age and medical history. 

The legal team is currently exploring options to halt the deportation process, citing humanitarian grounds and the risk of exposure to Ebola in the DRC. The situation is further complicated by the fact that the DRC has been facing outbreaks of Ebola, which poses a significant public health risk.

### The Ebola Crisis in the DRC

Ebola is a severe and often fatal disease caused by the Ebola virus, which has been endemic in parts of Africa, including the DRC. The country has experienced multiple outbreaks over the years, with the most recent surge raising alarms among global health officials. The World Health Organization (WHO) and other health agencies have been actively monitoring the situation, emphasizing the need for caution and preparedness.

As of now, the DRC is implementing measures to control the spread of the virus, including vaccination campaigns and public health awareness initiatives. However, the risk remains high, particularly for vulnerable populations, such as the elderly or those with pre-existing health conditions.
